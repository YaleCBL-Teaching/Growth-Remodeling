\begin{frame}[t]{Skip the transient --- solve for the end state}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
      Often you do not care \emph{how} the tissue reaches its adapted state --- only
      \emph{what} that state is. The equilibrated model sets every rate to zero and
      solves the resulting \textbf{algebraic} system: one root-find instead of
      thousands of time steps.
      \vspace{0.5em}

      At mechanobiological equilibrium:
      \begin{itemize}\small
        \item[(A)] growth balance, $\Upsilon=1$: $\ \bar\sigma^*=\bar\sigma_h$;
        \item[(B)] remodeling done: $\ \lambda_e^k=G^k\Rightarrow\sigma^k=\sigma_h^k$;
        \item[(C)] mechanical equilibrium at the evolved geometry (Laplace).
      \end{itemize}
      Elastin cannot remodel: its mass is the surviving fraction $s$, its stress
      follows the evolved stretch.
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \slidefig[\linewidth]{equil_longterm.png}
      \figcredit{Fig.: M. Pfaller (BMES 2023); Latorre, Humphrey, ZAMM (2018)}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]{Collapse to one scalar equation}
  Let the turnover constituents grow in fixed proportion (exact when they share
  gain and turnover). Conditions (A)--(C) reduce to a \emph{single} equation for
  the evolved stretch $\lambda^*$:
  \slboxeq{2.8em}{eq:equil}{\phi_{e0}\,s
    + s\,\frac{\bar\sigma_h - \sigma_e(G_e\lambda^*)}{\sigma_{\text{turn}} - \sigma_h^e}
    - \gamma\,(\lambda^*)^{n} = 0}
  with $n$ the geometry exponent (1 bar, 2 artery), $\gamma$ the sustained load
  factor, $s$ the surviving elastin fraction. Once $\lambda^*$ is known, the
  evolved mass ratio is $M/M_0=\gamma(\lambda^*)^n$.
  \vspace{0.4em}

  Because (6.1) is just the fixed point of the homogenized ODEs, the equilibrated
  solution \textbf{coincides with the long-time limit of the transient theories}
  --- to several digits.
  \srccite{Latorre, Humphrey, ZAMM (2018)}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]{The punchline: existence = stability}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
      Equation \eqref{eq:equil} may have \textbf{no physical root}. Then there is
      \emph{no} adapted state to converge to --- and the transient theories dilate
      without bound. So the equilibrated solver is also the cleanest possible
      \textbf{stability test}: one algebraic equation; a root $\Rightarrow$ the
      tissue adapts, no root $\Rightarrow$ mechanobiologically unstable.
      \vspace{0.4em}

      {\color{AccentRed} Learning: the equilibrated model \emph{matches} the
      transient theories \emph{if} an equilibrium exists --- and, by failing to
      find one, flags exactly when it does not.}
      \vspace{0.4em}

      Note the \textbf{critical slowing down}: the closer the insult to the
      boundary, the longer the transient takes --- which is why the instant test
      is so useful.
    \end{column}

\end{frame}
