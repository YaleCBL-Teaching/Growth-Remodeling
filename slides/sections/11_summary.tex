\begin{frame}[t]{Summary --- what to take away}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Living tissue continuously \textbf{turns over}; cells deposit new
    material \emph{pre-stretched}, chasing a homeostatic mechanical set-point.
    \item \textbf{Kinematic growth} imposes that set-point --- simple and robust,
    but stability is assumed, not predicted.
    \item \textbf{Constrained mixtures} predict stability from turnover, and it
    \textbf{depends on the insult}: mild $\to$ adapt, severe $\to$ runaway.
    \item The \textbf{homogenized} model reproduces the full model's time
    trajectory at a fraction of the cost.
    \item The \textbf{equilibrated} model matches the transient theories \emph{when
    an equilibrium exists}, and flags instability by failing to find one.
    \item These tools now drive \textbf{clinical prediction} (TEVGs),
    \textbf{systems-biology coupling}, and \textbf{fluid--solid--growth}.
  \end{enumerate}
  \vfill
  \begin{center}\color{AccentRed}
  \emph{When does a loaded tissue adapt, and when does it run away? --- now you can
  compute the answer.}
  \end{center}
\end{frame}
