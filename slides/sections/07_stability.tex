\begin{frame}[t]{Where Instability Comes From: The Laplace Exponent}
  For a pressurised tube the stress the wall \emph{must} carry scales with radius,
  and radius grows with mass. Write the required (Laplace) stress as a power of the
  stretch, $\bar\sigma_{\text{req}}\propto\lambda^{\,n}$, and use the equilibrium
  mass--stretch relation $M/M_0=\gamma\,\lambda^{\,n}$:
  \giveneq{\text{bar: }n=1\ (\text{tension}=Pa),\qquad
    \text{artery: }n=2\ (\bar\sigma=\tfrac{Pa}{h},\ a\!\propto\!\lambda,\ h\!\propto\!M).}
  Differentiate the growth balance at equilibrium. Adding mass \emph{lowers} the
  required stress only if the geometric feedback stays sub-linear:
  \slboxeq{2.2em}{eq:stab}{\left.\frac{\dd\bar\sigma}{\dd\theta}\right|_{\theta^*}<0
    \ \Longleftrightarrow\ n<n_{\text{crit}}\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \text{tissue adapts}}
  \textbf{Bar} ($n=1$): always stable. \textbf{Artery} ($n=2$, the Laplace
  exponent): stable only while enough elastin survives; lose elastin and the
  quadratic term wins --- $\dd\bar\sigma/\dd\theta>0$, \textbf{runaway}.
  \begin{center}\footnotesize\color{BoxGray}
  The whole stability verdict follows analytically from the Laplace exponent $n$
  --- no time integration needed. (Explored numerically in \texttt{ex06}.)
  \end{center}
  \srccite{Cyron, Wilson, Humphrey, J R Soc Interface (2014)}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]{Same Tissue, Two Insults}
  Read stability two ways: \textbf{existence} --- the equilibrated equation
  \eqref{eq:equil} has a root iff the tissue can adapt (no time integration); or
  \textbf{transient} --- integrate and watch it plateau or climb. The same tissue
  can do either: mild elastin loss adapts, severe loss dilates without bound.
\end{frame}
