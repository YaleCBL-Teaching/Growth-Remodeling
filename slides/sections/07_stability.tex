\begin{frame}[t]{Where Instability Comes From: The Laplace Exponent}
  In a pressurised tube the wall stress is $\bar\sigma=\tfrac{Pa}{h}$ with radius
  $a\!\propto\!\lambda$ and thickness $h\!\propto\!M$, so the required (Laplace)
  stress is \emph{quadratic} in the stretch,
  $\bar\sigma_{\text{req}}\propto\gamma\,\lambda^{2}/M$. The homeostatic state is
  stable only if adding mass \emph{lowers} the stress it must carry:
  \slboxeq{eq:stab}{\left.\frac{\dd\bar\sigma}{\dd\theta}\right|_{\theta^*}<0
    \ \Longleftrightarrow\ \text{tissue adapts}}
  \begin{columns}[c]
    \begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
      \begin{center}\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig00_required_stress.pdf}\end{center}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.54\textwidth}
      \small
      Growth thickens the wall ($h\!\propto\!M$), which lowers stress; but
      dilation raises it as $\lambda^2$ (Laplace).
      While enough \textbf{elastin} survives, its stiff recoil keeps
      $\dd\bar\sigma/\dd\theta<0$ and the artery \textbf{adapts}. Lose enough
      elastin and the sign flips, $\dd\bar\sigma/\dd\theta>0$:
      \textbf{runaway}. It is the quadratic Laplace exponent that makes this
      possible.
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
  \begin{center}\footnotesize\color{BoxGray}
  The verdict follows analytically from the Laplace feedback (curve is
  reproducible; explored numerically in \texttt{ex05}).
  \end{center}
  \srccite{Cyron, Wilson, Humphrey, J R Soc Interface (2014)}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]{Same Tissue, Two Insults}
  Read stability two ways: \textbf{existence}: the equilibrated equation
  \eqref{eq:equil} has a root iff the tissue can adapt (no time integration); or
  \textbf{transient}: integrate and watch it plateau or climb. The same tissue
  can do either: mild elastin loss adapts, severe loss dilates without bound.
\end{frame}

    \srun{\scodeline{uv run python run.py configs/ex05\_stability.yaml}

    \sstudy{Enlarge the \emph{adapts} region by giving the wall more load-bearing
      capacity, then compare the printed fraction:
      \scodeline{... --set model.constituents.collagen.law.c1=600}
      \scodeline{... --set model.constituents.collagen.G=1.15}
      (Raising the \texttt{gain} does \emph{not} move this map: existence is
      gain-independent. Gain governs \emph{dynamic} stability instead, in the
      transient.)}
  \end{setupbox}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]{}
  \begin{answerbox}{Stability map}
    Stiffer collagen or a larger deposition stretch enlarge the \emph{adapts}
    region: the wall carries more load, so it tolerates more elastin loss. The
    \texttt{gain} leaves this existence map unchanged (at equilibrium, production
    balances removal and the gain cancels); it instead sets \emph{dynamic}
    stability, whether the transient actually reaches an equilibrium that exists
    (too weak adaptation runs away even when a root exists).
  \end{answerbox}

  enough to \emph{reach} it, existence is necessary, sufficient gain makes it so.\end{center}
\end{frame}
